Urge surfing
Observe it without acting on it.
Step 1. Locate it.
Where do you feel the urge in your body? Chest, stomach, hands? Just notice where it lives. Don't push it away.
Step 2. Describe it.
Tight? Buzzing? Heavy? Give it a shape and texture. The moment you describe it you are observing it, not controlled by it.
Step 3. Ride the wave.
Urges always peak and fade like a wave. You are not fighting it. You are surfing it. Keep breathing. It will pass.
Step 4. Notice you survived it.
When it fades and it will, notice that you didn't act on it. That is the skill. You just did it.

Why the math is always against you
Interactive. See it for yourself.
The gambler's fallacy
Your brain is wired to find patterns. When you see heads, heads, heads, heads — your gut says tails is due. It is not. Each flip is completely independent of the last. The coin has no memory. Neither does a slot machine, a roulette wheel, or a betting market.

The house edge
Every game is mathematically designed so the house keeps a percentage over time. American roulette has a 5.26% house edge. Slot machines average 5 to 15%. Sports books keep 4 to 8% on every dollar wagered. No system beats this long-term. The longer you play the more certain your loss becomes.
Why occasional wins feel so powerful
Unpredictable occasional rewards are the most powerful behavioral trap in psychology. It's the same mechanism that makes social media addictive. Gambling is engineered around this. The wins are real. The system is designed so losses outpace them over time.
